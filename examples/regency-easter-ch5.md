> **Regency Easter** — Regency Romance, ~30K words

> *Alistair Holt proposed to Eliza eight years ago at a crowded ball. She laughed. The room laughed with him. He hasn't been earnest about anything since. Now they're both guests at an Easter house party in the Cotswolds, and a rainstorm has driven them both to the chapel.*

# Chapter 5

The rain came on the fourth day like a judgement.

It had been building since morning — a thickening of the sky, a heaviness in the air, the particular quality of light that turns everything the colour of pewter. By noon the first drops were falling, fat and deliberate, and by two o'clock the world beyond Thornfield's windows had dissolved into a grey curtain of water that showed no intention of stopping.

The house turned inward. Frances produced card games and conversation and enough tea to float a barge. The drawing room filled with the gentle sounds of a rainy afternoon — the slap of cards, the murmur of talk, the percussion of rain against glass. Sir George told a story about a hunt that had gone wrong in weather like this. Mrs. Dalrymple embroidered. James read, undisturbed, his spaniel asleep at his feet.

Alistair could not settle.

He tried the library. He tried the morning room. He tried standing at the window and watching the rain as though weather were interesting, which it was not. The house felt smaller than it had the day before — the walls closer, the rooms more contained, the knowledge of exactly where Eliza was at any given moment pressing against his awareness like a low, persistent sound he could not block out.

She was in the drawing room. He could hear her voice — not the words, just the cadence, the particular rhythm of her speech when she was being charming and not quite meaning it. He left the window and went back to the library and sat in a chair and opened a book and read the same paragraph four times without absorbing a word.

This was ridiculous. He was a grown man in a well-appointed house with a book and a fire and no reason to be anywhere other than exactly where he was. The walk yesterday had been — he searched for the right word and rejected every option that implied significance. The walk had been a walk. She had said a few things that were honest. He had said a few things that were honest. This happened between people. It did not mean anything.

The problem was that he could not stop thinking about the way she had looked at him on the hillside — that unguarded moment, stripped of performance, when she had looked at him as if she were deciding whether he was safe. The question had been visible in her face, and the vulnerability of it — of asking, without words, whether the man who had every reason to despise her might be someone she could trust — had reached through every wall he'd built and touched something he had thought was dead.

It was not dead. This was extremely inconvenient.

He stood up, left the library, and went to find his coat.

He told himself he wanted air. He told himself the rain was not as bad as it looked from inside, that a walk in weather was a form of discipline, that the restlessness in his legs had nothing to do with the restlessness elsewhere. He told himself he was not going to the chapel. He told himself, specifically, that he was not going to the chapel because he had mentioned the stained glass to her yesterday and she had said she wanted to see it, and the chapel was exactly where a person who wanted to see stained glass in a rainstorm might go, and he was not — he was *not* — going to look for her.

He went to find his coat.

---

The garden was a disaster. The paths had become streams, the flower beds were drowning, and the ornamental hedges looked like they were contemplating surrender. Alistair walked through it anyway, coat pulled tight, the rain finding every gap in his clothing with the methodical persistence of water that had nowhere better to be.

He walked east. Not toward the ridge — toward the chapel. The decision had apparently been made somewhere below the level of conscious thought, in whatever part of him had stopped taking orders from the discipline. He was halfway across the grounds when it became clear that this was not a walk so much as an admission — a physical enactment of the principle that he was going to do the thing he had told himself he was not going to do, and the telling had been, from the beginning, a formality. The rain was coming harder now, sheeting across the hillside, turning the path to mud. His boots were ruined. His coat was inadequate. He did not care.

He saw the chapel through the rain — a grey shape on the eastern edge of the grounds, half hidden by a stand of yew trees. Small, old, built from the same Cotswold stone as the house but rougher, darker, the stone worn smooth by centuries of weather. The door was heavy oak. He pushed it open and stepped inside.

The silence hit him first. After the rain's relentless noise, the chapel's quiet was almost physical — a weight, a substance, the kind of silence that has depth. Then the cold, which was the cold of stone that has never been warm. Then the light: pale and coloured, filtered through the stained glass that James's grandfather had installed — the panels depicting lilies, a lamb, something he thought was meant to be resurrection but looked, in the grey light, more like bewilderment. The eastern window held the blue that James had described at breakfast — the ground lapis, mixed to order, a shade somewhere between the sky and the sea that had no business being in a Cotswold chapel and was, for all that, exactly right. It fell across the stone floor in a long, blue-gold rectangle that looked, Alistair thought, like a door into somewhere else.

And then Eliza.

She was sitting in the second pew, her shawl dark with rain, her hair escaping its pins where the water had loosened it. She was not reading. She was not praying. She was sitting very still, looking at the stained glass with the kind of attention that is not really looking at anything at all, and when the door opened and the sound of the rain rushed in, she turned and saw him and her face did something that was not the usual mask and was not surprise and was, he thought, closer to resignation — the look of a woman who had come here to be alone and had failed at even that.

"Mr. Holt," she said.

"Mrs. Ashworth." He stood in the doorway, dripping. "I seem to have underestimated the weather."

"As did I." She looked at him — at his sodden coat, his ruined boots, the rain running down his face — and something shifted in her expression. "You're soaked through."

"An astute observation."

"Come in. You'll catch your death standing in the doorway."

He came in. He closed the door. The rain became distant — still audible, a steady percussion on the stone roof, but contained, outside, belonging to a world they were temporarily exempt from. The chapel was small — eight pews, a simple altar, a stone floor that held the cold like a promise. He sat down in the pew across the aisle from her, because sitting beside her would imply something, and standing would be absurd, and leaving was not, apparently, an option his body was willing to consider.

They sat. The rain drummed above them. The coloured light from the stained glass fell across the floor between them in fragments — blue, gold, a deep, sacramental red.

"I didn't know this was here," she said, after a silence that could have been a minute or ten. "Until yesterday. Frances mentioned it, and I thought — in this weather —"

"A place to hide."

She looked at him. "Yes."

The honesty of it was startling. Not the word — the fact that she'd said it to him. That she had named, without deflection, what she was doing here: hiding. From the house, from the company, from the relentless requirement to be charming and composed and present. She was hiding, and she had told him so, and the trust implicit in that admission was so at odds with everything he thought he knew about her that he did not know where to put it.

"I used to come here as a boy," he said. "When the house was too much."

"Was it often too much?"

"Often enough." He leaned back in the pew. The wood was smooth and cold against his shoulders. "My father was — a presence. The kind of man who filled a room by being in it. He didn't need to raise his voice. You simply felt the weight of him, and adjusted yourself accordingly."

"And you came here."

"I came here. Sat in this pew, usually. Listened to the rain or the wind or whatever was happening outside, and waited until the weight lifted."

"Did it?"

"Not always. But the waiting was better here than in the house."

She was quiet for a while. The rain continued — a little softer now, or maybe he was just used to it. The chapel held them in its cold, still space, and the ordinary rules of engagement — the banter, the politeness, the careful monitoring of every word for exposure — seemed to have been suspended by mutual, unspoken agreement. There was no audience. There was no performance. There were two people in a stone building while the rain fell, and the simplicity of it was almost unbearable.

"The blue is extraordinary," she said, looking up at the eastern window. The ground lapis panel cast its light across the floor between them — a long, luminous rectangle that shifted as the clouds moved outside, sometimes bright, sometimes dim, like a living thing. "James told me his grandfather commissioned it from a glazier in London. Had the pigment ground specially."

"He was particular, the grandfather. Built the chapel himself — well, paid for it. The house was already a hundred years old, but he thought it needed a place of worship that was separate from the parish church. Somewhere private."

"Somewhere to hide."

"Precisely."

She smiled — the small, private one, the one that surfaced before her defences could catch it. "I suppose every family needs a room like that. A place where the rules are different."

"Frances would say the rules are the same everywhere. She simply changes them to suit."

"Frances," Eliza said, and the warmth in her voice when she said the name was unguarded, "is the most extraordinary person I know. She has the gift of making everyone feel as though they belong wherever she has put them."

"She has the gift of putting people wherever she wants them and making it look like their idea."

"That too." A pause. "She loves you very much."

The observation landed with unexpected weight. He looked at her — she was still watching the blue light on the floor, her profile lit by it, her face quiet — and he thought: *she is telling me something. Not about Frances. About what it looks like from the outside, to watch someone love a person who will not let themselves be loved.*

He let the silence hold.

"May I ask you something?" she said.

"You may ask."

"After — what happened. At the Harrington ball." She said it carefully, as if she were handling something that might cut her. "What did you do?"

He had not expected this. The question landed in a place he kept sealed, and for a moment the seal held — the instinct to deflect, to offer something dry and dismissive, to perform the man who had moved on. But the chapel would not permit it. The space was too honest. The rain was too close.

"I left London," he said. "For a year. Went to Hampshire. Managed the estate. Did not attend a single ball, dinner, or social occasion of any kind. Frances wrote me letters. I did not reply to most of them."

"A year."

"A year. And when I came back, I was —" He searched for the word. "Different. Finished, in a way. With whatever part of me had been foolish enough to stand in a ballroom and say what I felt without checking first whether it would be welcome."

The silence after this was long. He could hear her breathing. He could hear his own. The coloured light had shifted — the sun was moving behind the clouds, and the fragments of blue and gold on the floor were fading.

"I'm sorry," she said.

He looked at her. She was looking at her hands, which were folded in her lap, very still. Her face was — he didn't have a word for it. Not the controlled surface. Not the performance. Something underneath both, something he had never seen, something that looked like it cost her a great deal to let him see.

"You've never said that before," he said.

"No."

"In eight years."

"No." She raised her eyes. They were dark and steady and completely undefended, and the impact of them was like stepping off a ledge. "I should have said it much sooner. I should have said a great many things much sooner."

The sentence hung in the air. *A great many things.* He felt the pull of it — the implication that there were things unsaid, things he didn't know, a version of events he had not been given. The question formed — *what things?* — and he felt its weight, and he let it go. Not because he didn't want to know. Because knowing, in this chapel, in this rain, with her face looking like that, would require him to dismantle something he was not yet ready to dismantle.

"We should go back," he said. "The rain's easing."

She looked at him for a moment longer — long enough that he felt the question she was not asking press against the question he was not asking, two silences leaning against each other in the dark — and then she nodded.

"Yes. I suppose we should."

They stood. The pews released them. The chapel, which had held them in its cold, truthful space for an hour, returned to being a building — stone, wood, glass, nothing more. Alistair opened the heavy door and the world rushed back in: the smell of wet earth, the sound of dripping trees, the pale grey light of an English afternoon.

They walked back to the house in silence. The rain had softened to a mist — fine and persistent, blurring the garden hedges and the stone walls, making the house ahead of them look like something seen through gauze. Their footsteps fell into the same rhythm they had found on the ridge, the unconscious synchronisation that felt, each time it happened, less like accident and more like grammar — the syntax of two people whose bodies had decided something their minds had not yet ratified.

It was not the managed silence of the first days, not the charged silence of the walk along the ridge. It was a silence that was full — of things said and things not said, of a question deferred, of two people who had stepped outside the architecture of their enmity and were not sure how to step back in. The chapel had changed the terms. Before it, they had been opponents. After it, they were something else — something that had no name yet but that occupied the space between enmity and trust, a territory neither of them had mapped.

At the garden door, she stopped.

"Thank you," she said.

"For what?"

"For telling me about your father. And for — being here, I suppose. When I was hiding."

He should have said something light. Something dismissive. Something that reasserted the distance between them and restored the clean, cold lines of the fortress.

"You don't need to hide from me," he said.

The words came out before he could stop them, and they were true, and he had no idea when they had become true, and the look she gave him in response — surprised, almost wounded, as if kindness from him was a thing she had not prepared for — broke something in his chest that he had not known was still intact.

She went inside.

He stood in the garden doorway, rain dripping from his coat, and stared at the place where she had been, and thought: *I am in trouble.*

Not the kind of trouble that has a solution. Not the kind that can be managed by discipline, or distance, or the careful construction of walls.

The other kind.
