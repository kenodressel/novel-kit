> **The Republic of Letters** — Historical Fiction, ~45K words
>
> *Charlotte Ashworth has spent eight years believing Edward Hale abandoned her without a word. Now, trapped together at a house party, he's about to tell her what actually happened — and why he never came back.*

# Chapter Eight

He told her.

Not the careful version. Not the measured, patient version he had been preparing since the invitation arrived, the one in which he was honest but controlled, where the truth was released in doses calibrated to minimise damage. That version was gone. Charlotte had looked at him with those grey eyes — the ones that saw too much, the ones that had always been able to strip him to his bones — and said *Tell me*, and the careful version had dissolved like salt in water, and what was left was the truth, all of it, ugly and unmanaged.

"My father owed twenty thousand pounds," Edward said. "Gambling debts, mostly. Some bad investments. By the time I was eighteen, the estate was encumbered beyond recovery. We were going to lose everything — the house, the land, the title in all but name."

He was standing by the window. Charlotte was at the desk. The Persian carpet between them might as well have been the English Channel.

"Your mother approached my father. Or he approached her — I never learned which, and it may not matter. The arrangement was financial. The Ashworth family would provide the funds to stabilise the Hale barony. In exchange—" He stopped. His hand went to the back of his neck — a gesture that was not polished or social but raw, the gesture of a man in physical discomfort. "In exchange, I was to be removed."

"Removed," Charlotte said. The word was flat. Clinical. The tone of a woman repeating evidence.

"A naval commission was purchased. I was told on a Tuesday and on the ship by Friday. I had no say. I was nineteen. My father had the legal authority to commit me and he used it." Edward's jaw tightened. "I did not know about the financial arrangement at the time. I didn't know your mother was involved. I knew only that my father had decided I was going to sea and that no argument I could make would change his mind."

"And you didn't tell me."

"I couldn't reach you. I was given three days. My father kept me in the house — he was not a cruel man in most things, but in this he was ruthless, because he was desperate. I was not permitted visitors. I was not permitted to send messages. I wrote you a letter the night before I left and gave it to a footman and I don't know if it was ever sent."

"It wasn't," Charlotte said. "I never received anything."

Edward closed his eyes. Opened them. "I wrote from the ship. The first letter was from Portsmouth, before we sailed. I wrote twenty-three letters in the first two years — from Portsmouth, from Gibraltar, from Malta, from everywhere the Navy sent me. I addressed them to you. I sent them to the Ashworth house." His voice was steady but his hands were not — Charlotte saw them, curled at his sides, the roughened fingers pressing into his palms. "I never received a reply. Not one word. In two years."

"And you concluded I didn't care."

"I concluded—" He paused. When he spoke again, the steadiness had cost him something. "I concluded that whatever I had imagined between us had been one-sided. That you had moved on. That I had been a boy with a fantasy and you had been a girl with better options. I stopped writing in the third year. I stopped hoping."

Charlotte said nothing. The rain hammered the windows. The fire popped and settled. The library was dim and close and honest, and Charlotte sat in it and absorbed the full shape of the conspiracy that had eaten her life, and she did not cry, because she was Charlotte Ashworth and she did not cry, and because she was saving her tears for someone who deserved them and Edward was not yet on that list.

"When did you find out about my mother?" she asked.

Edward was quiet for a moment. "The second year. My brother wrote to me — Charles, before he died. One of the few letters that reached me. He mentioned, in passing, that 'the Ashworth matter' was 'resolved to everyone's satisfaction.' It was a single line. A footnote. I read it a hundred times."

"And you understood."

"I understood that the arrangement involved your family. That my commission and your mother's cooperation were connected. I didn't know the full extent — whether she initiated it, whether she simply consented — but I knew she was part of it."

Charlotte's voice, when she spoke, was very quiet. It was the quietness that Mary would have recognised as the most dangerous register Charlotte possessed — not the cold precision of her social voice, not the sharp edge of her wit, but the flat, soft voice that meant she had passed through anger and arrived at something worse.

"You knew about my mother," she said. "Before you came here."

"Yes."

"For years."

"Yes."

"And you didn't tell me."

The question was simple. The silence that followed it was not.

Edward looked at her. Charlotte watched his face cycle through several expressions — guilt, shame, the particular misery of a man who has just been asked the one question he cannot answer well — and settle on something that was, at least, honest.

"I was protecting you," he said.

Charlotte laughed. It was not the involuntary, surprised laugh from the garden walk, the one that had cracked open a two-second window into the Charlotte underneath the armour. This was a different kind of laugh entirely — short, sharp, utterly humourless. The laugh of a woman who has just heard the one thing she cannot bear to hear, delivered by the one person she most needed not to hear it from.

"Protecting me," she repeated.

"I know how it sounds—"

"It sounds like my mother. It sounds exactly like my mother." Charlotte stood. The chair scraped against the floor. "She removed you from my life to *protect* me from an unsuitable attachment. She intercepted your letters to *protect* me from false hopes. She arranged my future without my knowledge or consent to *protect* me from making choices she disapproved of. And you — *you* — sat on the truth about her involvement for years, because you were *protecting* me from information I had a right to. Do you see the pattern, Edward? Because I see it very clearly."

She had called him Edward. Not Lord Hale. She had not noticed, and he had.

"Charlotte—"

"You did the same thing she did. You decided what I could handle. You chose for me. You looked at the truth and you decided — on my behalf, without asking — that I was better off not knowing." Her voice had risen. Not to a shout — Charlotte did not shout. But the flatness was gone, replaced by something raw and live, something that had been compressed for years and was now expanding, filling the room. "You had a truth that was mine. It was *mine*, Edward. My mother, my letters, my stolen years. And you kept it because you decided I was too fragile, or too angry, or too something to be trusted with my own life."

"I didn't think you were fragile—"

"Then why?"

The question hung in the rain-light. Edward stood by the window and Charlotte stood by the desk and the distance between them was a Persian carpet and nine years and the particular, devastating gap between wanting to be honest and actually being honest.

"Because I was afraid," Edward said.

Charlotte went still.

"I was afraid that if I told you — if I told you about your mother, about the arrangement, about all of it — you would hate me for being the reason it happened. That you would look at me and see the price your family paid, and that the hatred would be—" His voice broke. Repaired itself. Continued, rougher. "I was afraid the truth would be the thing that made you never want to see me again. And I had already lived nine years without you, and I could not—"

He stopped.

"I was a coward," he said. "I was afraid. That is the whole of it."

Charlotte stared at him. He looked back at her with an expression that was no longer charm or patience or the careful social mask. It was just Edward. Stripped of everything. A man standing in a library admitting the worst thing about himself to the person whose opinion mattered most.

"You were afraid," Charlotte said.

"Yes."

"And so you waited. Three years with the title. Three years with the money. Three years during which you could have walked to my door and told me everything. And instead you waited for a house party invitation and a coincidence."

"I didn't know you would be here. The duchess—"

"The duchess manoeuvred us both, yes. But you could have come at any time. You had three years, Edward. *Three years.* And instead of coming to me — instead of writing to me again, or calling, or sending a message, or doing any of the hundred things a man with a title and a fortune and the full truth can do — you stayed away. Because you were afraid."

"Yes." The word was barely audible.

"Your fear cost me nine years."

The sentence landed in the room like a stone dropped into still water. It rippled outward, silent and enormous, and in its wake the library was very quiet.

Edward had no answer. Charlotte could see him searching for one — see the muscles of his jaw working, the way his hands opened and closed at his sides, the discipline it was taking him not to reach for words that would soften the blow. He did not reach. He did not soften. He stood there and absorbed it, the way he had absorbed her anger in the garden, the way he absorbed everything she threw at him — not because he thought he could weather it, but because he knew he deserved it.

"I know," he said. "I know. I have no defence."

"No," Charlotte agreed. "You don't."

She looked at him for a long moment. The rain. The grey light. The fire dying in the grate. Edward's face, stripped of everything she had hated about it — the charm, the ease, the social confidence — and showing only what was underneath: a man who had failed the woman he loved and knew it and was not, for once, trying to make it better with words.

Charlotte turned toward the door.

"Charlotte—"

"Don't."

She walked to the door. She put her hand on the handle. She paused.

"I believe you," she said, without turning around. "About the letters. About what they did. I believe all of it. And I am angrier at my mother than I have ever been at anyone in my life, and that includes you, which should tell you something about the scale of what she has done."

She paused.

"But you had the truth, and you kept it. And I need—" Her voice caught. A tiny fracture, barely audible, immediately controlled. "I need to not be near you right now."

She opened the door. She stepped through it. She closed it behind her — quietly, with the precise, deliberate care of a woman who understood that quiet doors were louder than slammed ones.

Edward stood alone in the library.

---

The rain continued.

Edward did not move from the window. The light was grey and flat and the water streaked the glass in patterns he was not seeing because he was not looking at the glass. He was looking at the door Charlotte had closed — quietly, devastatingly, with the soft click that was worse than any slam because a slam was an emotion and a quiet click was a decision.

*Your fear cost me nine years.*

He sat down. Not in a chair — on the floor, his back against the bookshelves, his legs stretched out across the Persian carpet that Charlotte had stood on and looked at him from, and he put his head in his hands.

He had been afraid. It was the truth, and it was not enough, and the insufficiency of it was the point. He had spent nine years constructing a narrative about himself — the man who had been taken, the man who had written letters, the man who had been wronged — and Charlotte had looked at that narrative and asked the one question that destroyed it: *Why didn't you come sooner?* And the answer — fear, just fear, nothing noble or complicated, just the ordinary human failure of a man who was afraid of what he'd find — was the answer of a man who did not deserve the woman he loved.

He had not been taken from Charlotte. He had been taken, yes, at nineteen — that part was real, that part was not his fault. But at twenty-five, when his brother died and the title came and the fortune with it, he had been a free man. A man with means and status and every resource that the world could provide. And he had used those resources to stay away. To tell himself that Charlotte had moved on. To protect himself from the possibility that she hadn't, because if she hadn't, then her silence was not indifference but suffering, and her suffering was something he had caused by not coming back.

He had chosen comfort. He had chosen the version of the story where Charlotte was fine, where she had married or moved on or forgotten, where the damage was historical and contained. He had chosen this because the alternative — that Charlotte was waiting, that Charlotte was hurting, that Charlotte had spent nine years building walls because the person who should have come back for her had been too afraid to try — the alternative was unbearable.

And now Charlotte had stood in front of him and held up a mirror, and the mirror showed not the patient, honest man he had been performing for three days but the man underneath: a coward. A man who had let fear make his decisions for three years, the same way Charlotte's mother had let self-interest make hers for nine.

*Protecting you.* He had said he was protecting her, and Charlotte had laughed — that terrible, humourless laugh — and said *It sounds like my mother.*

She was right. She was always right. That was the thing about Charlotte — her anger was so precise, so surgically targeted, that you could not dismiss it. She did not exaggerate. She did not dramatise. She simply identified the truth and held it up, and the truth did the damage all on its own.

Edward sat on the library floor and listened to the rain and thought about the letters he had written. Twenty-three letters. Sent from ships and ports and borrowed desks in places Charlotte had never seen. Letters that had been intercepted and destroyed by a woman who believed she was doing the right thing, and letters that Charlotte had never known existed until this week, when she had put together the pieces herself — without his help, because he had not offered it.

She had done it alone. She had always done it alone. That was the tragedy underneath the tragedy — that Charlotte Ashworth, who had been failed by every person who was supposed to protect her, had learned to protect herself with such devastating competence that she no longer believed anyone else was capable of it.

And he had proved her right.

Edward pressed the heels of his hands against his eyes. He breathed. The rain kept falling.

He thought about what Charlotte had said at the door: *I believe you. About the letters. About what they did.* She believed him. She was angrier at her mother than at him, and this was not a comfort but a fact, and the fact contained a thread of something that Edward held onto with the desperation of a man in deep water: Charlotte had not shut the door forever. She had shut it for now. She needed time. She needed space. She needed to not be near him.

He would give her that. He would give her whatever she needed, for as long as she needed it, because this was the lesson he was learning — not the grand lesson about honesty or courage, but the smaller, harder lesson about the difference between what he wanted to give Charlotte and what Charlotte actually needed.

What Charlotte needed was not his explanations or his patience or his carefully managed truth. What Charlotte needed was to be trusted with the whole of it — the ugly, unmanageable whole — and to be allowed to decide, on her own terms, what to do with it.

He had not given her that. He had given her the palatable version and held back the rest, and she had gone out and found the rest on her own, and the finding had been its own kind of wound — because every truth she'd uncovered was a truth he could have given her freely, and his failure to give it had been one more person deciding what Charlotte could handle.

He would not make that mistake again.

Edward sat on the library floor until the fire went out. Then he rose, straightened his coat, ran a hand through his hair, and went to find Jennings, because he needed to dress for dinner and he needed to face a room full of people and he needed to do it knowing that the woman he loved was somewhere in this house, hurting, and that the only thing he could do — the only thing she had asked him to do — was stay away.

He dressed for dinner. He went downstairs. He smiled at people.

It was the hardest thing he had done since leaving the Navy.
